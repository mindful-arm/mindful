Frequently asked questions

Can I get a second opinion from a different psychiatrist?

Yes. Seeking a second opinion is a normal and beneficial step if you’d like to explore alternative treatment approaches or confirm a diagnosis. At Mindful, we can help you arrange a consultation with a different specialist within our team. Your comfort and confidence in your treatment plan are important to us.

Can a psychiatrist help me if I want to change or decrease my medication?

Yes. Psychiatrists are medical doctors who specialise in mental health, which means they can evaluate and adjust your medication. If you’re considering changing, tapering, or discontinuing medication, a psychiatrist can provide expert guidance and monitor your progress safely.

What issues can psychiatry help with?

Psychiatry can address a wide range of mental health conditions, including mood difficulties, anxiety, sleep problems, attention difficulties, panic attacks, and more. Psychiatrists focus on both the psychological and medical aspects of mental well-being and offer appropriate support based on your needs.

Are psychiatry sessions confidential?

Yes. Confidentiality is essential in psychiatric practice, just as it is in psychological care. Information shared with your psychiatrist remains confidential, except when disclosure is required by law or necessary to prevent serious harm to yourself or others.

Do I need a GP referral to see a psychiatrist?

In many cases, you can self-refer for a private appointment. If you’re unsure whether you need a referral, you can contact us directly, and our team will help you understand the best option for your situation.

How long is a psychiatry session?

An initial psychiatric assessment often lasts around 50-60 minutes, allowing the psychiatrist to review your history, discuss symptoms, and formulate a treatment plan. Follow-up appointments may be shorter depending on your needs and progress.

Is a psychiatrist a therapist?

While psychiatrists can provide psychotherapeutic support, their primary role is often focused on diagnosis, medication management, and overseeing treatment from a medical standpoint. In some cases, they collaborate with psychologists or psychotherapists to provide comprehensive care.
