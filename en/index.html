Frequently asked questions

Why are there so many approaches to therapy?

There are many evidence-based approaches to therapy because different people and different difficulties need different kinds of support. The most important part is choosing an approach that fits your goals, symptoms, personality, and current life situation.

How do I know which therapy approach is right for me?

The right approach is usually chosen after an initial consultation. A specialist will listen to your concerns, understand your goals, and suggest a method or combination of methods that may be most helpful for you.

How do you decide which specialist I’ll be matched with?

The match is based on your needs, the type of difficulty you are facing, and the specialist’s experience. If during the first meeting it becomes clear that another approach or specialist would suit you better, the recommendation can be changed.

What can I expect during my first therapy session?

The first session is usually about getting to know you: your background, current concerns, expectations, and goals. You can ask questions, share what feels comfortable, and begin to understand what kind of support may be useful.

How long does therapy usually last?

The length of therapy depends on your goals, the complexity of the issue, and how the work develops. Some people need a short course of sessions, while others benefit from longer-term support.

Will therapy actually help me feel better?

Therapy can help by giving you space to understand yourself, notice patterns, develop coping skills, and work through emotional difficulties. Progress depends on consistency, openness, and the quality of the therapeutic relationship.

What if I’m nervous or uncomfortable opening up?

Feeling nervous at the beginning is completely normal. A good therapist will not pressure you to share everything at once. The process moves at a pace that feels safe and respectful for you.

Can I combine therapy with medication if needed?

Yes. For some people, therapy and medication can work well together. If medication may be helpful, a psychiatrist can assess this and collaborate with other specialists when needed.

Can therapy help if I’ve tried it before and it didn’t work?

Yes. A previous difficult or unhelpful therapy experience does not mean therapy cannot help. Sometimes a different specialist, approach, or pace can make the process more effective.

Is therapy suitable for couples, families, or children?

Yes. Therapy can be adapted for individuals, couples, families, children, and adolescents. The right format depends on the situation, the people involved, and the goals of the work.

Can I get a second opinion from a different psychiatrist?

Yes. Getting a second opinion can be helpful if you want to confirm a diagnosis, understand another treatment option, or feel more confident about your care plan.

Can a psychiatrist help me change or decrease my medication?

Yes. A psychiatrist can review your medication, discuss possible changes, and help you make adjustments safely. Medication should not be changed or stopped without professional guidance.

How much does a psychiatric assessment cost?

The cost can depend on the type and complexity of the assessment. It is best to contact the clinic directly for current pricing and to understand what kind of appointment you need.

What issues can psychiatry help with?

Psychiatry can help with many mental health difficulties, including mood problems, anxiety, attention difficulties, sleep issues, panic symptoms, and other conditions where medical assessment may be useful.

Are psychiatry sessions confidential?

Yes. Psychiatric sessions are confidential. Information is protected, with only limited exceptions required by law or serious safety concerns.

Do I need a GP referral to see a psychiatrist?

In many private settings, you can book directly. If you are unsure, it is best to contact the clinic and ask what is needed for your situation.

How long is a psychiatry session?

An initial psychiatric assessment often lasts around 50–60 minutes. Follow-up sessions are usually shorter and focus on progress, treatment planning, and any adjustments needed.

Is a psychiatrist a therapist?

A psychiatrist is a medical doctor who specialises in mental health. Some psychiatrists also provide therapy, but their main role often includes diagnosis, medication management, and medical treatment planning.
